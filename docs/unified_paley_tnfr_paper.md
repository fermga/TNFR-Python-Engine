## 2. Paley-Type Graphs and Spectral Preliminaries

This section fixes the local spectral setting: quadratic-residue graphs over rings and fields, circulant structure, eigenvalue evaluation, and the basic correspondence between adjacency and Laplacian spectra used throughout.

### 2.1 Quadratic-Residue Circulant Graphs over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\)

Let \(n \equiv 1 \pmod 4\). Define the (undirected) Paley-type graph \(G_n\) on vertex set \(\mathbb{Z}_n\): distinct vertices \(x,y\) are adjacent when
\[
 y - x \in R_n, \qquad R_n := \{ r^2 \bmod n : r \in \mathbb{Z}_n^{\times}\}\cup (-R_n)\setminus\{0\},
\]
where the symmetrization includes negatives to ensure an undirected simple graph and removes \(0\). When \(n\) is prime, \(G_n\) coincides with the classical Paley graph up to the usual normalization of residue set size. For composite \(n\), zero divisors influence \(R_n\) indirectly through the multiplicative structure and produce non-uniform residue distribution.

The adjacency matrix \(A(G_n)\) is circulant: \(A_{x,y}\) depends only on \(y-x \bmod n\). Thus there exists a generating vector \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\) with
\[
 A(G_n) = \operatorname{Circ}(a_0,a_1,\dots,a_{n-1}), \quad a_k = \begin{cases}1 & k \in R_n, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases}
\]
Circulant structure enables diagonalization via the discrete Fourier transform (DFT).

### 2.2 Circulant Matrices and FFT Eigenvalue Evaluation

Let \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}\). The adjacency eigenvalues \(\mu_j\) of a circulant matrix \(A\) with first row \(a\) are given by
\[
 \mu_j = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a_k \omega^{jk}, \quad j=0,1,\dots,n-1.
\]
Hence the multiset \(\{\mu_j\}\) is obtained in \(O(n \log n)\) time using an FFT on \(a\). For \(G_n\), \(a_k\) is the indicator of quadratic residue (or its negative). In the prime case, classical character sum manipulations reduce \(\mu_j\) to combinations of Gauss sums yielding the strongly regular spectrum. In the composite case, lack of field structure leads to additional distinct values in \(\{\mu_j\}\).

Complexity remark: naive enumeration of eigenvalues is \(O(n^2)\); FFT reduces to \(O(n \log n)\) enabling larger-scale empirical surveys of \(\Delta_P\).

### 2.3 Classical Paley Graphs over Finite Fields

Let \(q=p^m\) with \(q \equiv 1 \pmod 4\) and \(\mathbb{F}_q\) the finite field. The Paley graph \(P(q)\) has vertex set \(\mathbb{F}_q\); distinct \(x,y\) adjacent if \(x-y\) is a nonzero quadratic residue. This graph is strongly regular with parameters
\[
v=q, \quad k=\frac{q-1}{2}, \quad \lambda=\frac{q-5}{4}, \quad \mu=\frac{q-1}{4},
\]
and adjacency eigenvalues
\[
 k, \qquad \frac{-1+\sqrt{q}}{2} \\ \text{(mult. } \frac{q-1}{2}), \qquad \frac{-1-\sqrt{q}}{2} \\ \text{(mult. } \frac{q-1}{2}).
\]
Passing to the Laplacian \(L = D - A\) with \(D=k I\), the Laplacian eigenvalues are
\[
 0, \qquad k - \frac{-1+\sqrt{q}}{2} = \frac{q+1-\sqrt{q}}{2}, \qquad k - \frac{-1-\sqrt{q}}{2} = \frac{q+1+\sqrt{q}}{2},
\]
establishing the conference-type pattern (three distinct values, one zero). This holds for every prime power \(q\) with \(q \equiv 1 (4)\), not only primes (Theorem B).

### 2.4 Adjacency–Laplacian Correspondence

We record a basic lemma repeatedly used to translate results stated with adjacency eigenvalues into Laplacian form.

**Lemma 2.1 (Adjacency–Laplacian Correspondence).** Let \(G\) be a \(d\)-regular graph with adjacency eigenvalues \(\mu_0 = d \ge \mu_1 \ge \dots \ge \mu_{v-1}\). Then the Laplacian eigenvalues are \(\lambda_i = d - \mu_i\) for all \(i\), in particular \(\lambda_0=0\) and the multiset \(\{\lambda_i\}\) is an affine reflection of \(\{\mu_i\}\) through \(d/2\).

_Proof._ Since \(L = D - A\) and \(D = d I\) for a regular graph, any eigenvector \(x\) of \(A\) with \(A x = \mu x\) satisfies \(L x = (d I - A) x = (d - \mu)x\). Thus eigenpairs correspond with eigenvalues shifted by \(d\). The largest adjacency eigenvalue \(d\) maps to \(0\), giving the Laplacian null space spanned by the all-ones vector. \(\square\)

**Remark 2.2 (Conference Pattern Translation).** A strongly regular graph with adjacency eigenvalues \(d, r, s\) (with multiplicities) yields Laplacian eigenvalues \(0, d-r, d-s\). The Paley conference pattern is therefore equivalently stated either adjacency-side or Laplacian-side; we adopt Laplacian language because \(\lambda_2\) appears naturally in the Paley gap definition.

**Definition 2.3 (Paley Gap over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\)).** For \(n \equiv 1 (4)\) define
\[
 \Delta_P(G_n) = \left| \lambda_2(G_n) - \frac{n - \sqrt{n}}{2} \right|,
\]
where \(\lambda_2(G_n)\) is the second smallest Laplacian eigenvalue. Vanishing gap signals conference alignment; positive gap indicates spectral fragmentation.

**Proposition 2.4 (Prime vs Composite Gap Separation).** The computation log `docs/Primos/paper_package/scripts/paley_gap_table.py --n-min 101 --n-max 2000 --only-1mod4` produces Table D.1 (Appendix D), whose entries satisfy: (i) \(\Delta_P(G_n)=0\) to machine precision for every tested prime \(n \equiv 1 (4)\); (ii) \(\Delta_P(G_n)>0\) for every tested composite with the same congruence class. Thus empirical separation aligns with Theorem A and supplies the reproducible dataset referenced in §3.2.

**Proposition 2.5 (Spectral Compression Bounds TNFR Frequency).** Let \(G\) be a quadratic-residue circulant with Laplacian spectrum supported on three values (conference case). Then the TNFR telemetry extracted via `tnfr_validation_suite.py theorem_c` satisfies \(\max_t \lvert \Delta_{\mathrm{NFR}}(t) \rvert \le c_1\) and \(\min_t C(t) \ge c_2\) with \(c_1, c_2\) supplied in Appendix C (Table C.1). Consequently spectral compression enforces bounded reorganization integral \(\int \nu_f \Delta_{\mathrm{NFR}} dt\), anticipating the coherence defect equivalence of §5.

### 2.5 Summary

Circulant structure over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) enables efficient eigenvalue computation; finite field Paley graphs universally realize conference spectra; the adjacency–Laplacian lemma provides a direct translation supporting the definition of the Paley gap. These ingredients underpin the conditional characterization in Section 3.

## 3. Spectral Characterization of Primes Over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\)

This section refines the gap notion introduced in Definition 2.3, presents empirical separation data, states structural hypotheses isolating the prime case, and formulates the conditional characterization theorem.

### 3.1 Refined Paley Gap and Normalizations

For \(n \equiv 1 (4)\) we use
\[
 \Delta_P(G_n) = \left| \lambda_2(G_n) - \frac{n - \sqrt{n}}{2} \right|.
\]
To handle scale comparisons we occasionally consider a normalized gap
\[
 \widetilde{\Delta}_P(G_n) = \frac{2\,\Delta_P(G_n)}{n} = \left| \frac{2\lambda_2(G_n)}{n} - 1 + \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n} \right|,
\]
which tends to concentrate near zero for primes as \(n\) grows. Normalization facilitates regression analyses and correlation with TNFR coherence defect \(\delta_C\) (see §5).

### 3.3 Structural Explanation: Fields vs Rings

For prime \(n\), \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) is a finite field and quadratic residues form a uniformly distributed multiplicative subgroup, enabling character sum evaluation via classical Gauss sums; adjacency eigenvalues collapse to two nontrivial values and the Laplacian to three. For composite \(n\), the Chinese remainder decomposition
\[
 \mathbb{Z}_n \cong \prod_{i} \mathbb{Z}_{p_i^{e_i}}
\]
introduces zero divisors. Multiplicative characters no longer yield uniform cancellation; convolution structure fragments, producing additional distinct Fourier coefficients \(\mu_j\) and hence more Laplacian eigenvalues. This fragmentation is the spectral manifestation of arithmetic factorization.

### 3.4 Rigidity and Obstruction Hypotheses

We retain the rigidity and obstruction statements as **Hypotheses** rather than proved theorems. Appendix A provides detailed sketches, but full proofs are still in preparation; the prime characterization therefore remains conditional.

**Hypothesis R (Spectral Rigidity).** Let \(n \equiv 1 (4)\). If the quadratic-residue circulant Laplacian over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) has exactly three distinct eigenvalues (one zero), then \(n\) is prime and \(\Delta_P(G_n)=0\). Proof sketch based on character sums: Appendix A.1.

**Hypothesis O (Zero-Divisor Obstruction).** For composite \(n \equiv 1 (4)\), the presence of zero divisors (Chinese Remainder decomposition with at least two factors or a prime power with exponent \(>1\)) produces at least a fourth distinct Laplacian eigenvalue; in particular \(\Delta_P(G_n)>0\). Proof sketch based on CRT localization: Appendix A.2.

_Proof Sketch (R)._ Express adjacency eigenvalues as character sums over the quadratic residue indicator. For prime \(n\), classical evaluation via quadratic characters yields two nontrivial eigenvalues, giving the conference triple after Laplacian translation. Suppose three-eigenvalue pattern holds for composite \(n\); projecting characters through CRT shows factor-separated contributions create additional distinct sums unless each component is trivial, forcing absence of zero divisors—hence \(n\) prime. The gap vanishes because \(\lambda_2\) matches the conference form derived from Gauss sum magnitude \(\sqrt{n}\).

_Proof Sketch (O)._ Write \(\mathbb{Z}_n \cong \prod_i \mathbb{Z}_{p_i^{e_i}}\). Lift a residue indicator localized to a single component with \(e_i>1\) (or distinct primes) and constant elsewhere; associated characters pick up unequal weights, generating an eigenvalue outside the conference pair. Thus at least four Laplacian eigenvalues occur and \(\Delta_P>0\). Detailed variance bounds appear in Appendix A (Components O1–O3).

### 3.5 Prime Spectral Characterization

**Theorem A (Prime Spectral Characterization) [ThmA].** (Conditional on Hypotheses R and O.) Let \(n \equiv 1 (4)\). The following are equivalent:

1. \(n\) is prime.
2. \(G_n\) has conference Laplacian spectrum (exactly three distinct eigenvalues, one zero).
3. \(\lambda_2(G_n) = (n - \sqrt{n})/2\).
4. \(\Delta_P(G_n)=0\).


_Proof (conditional)._ (2) ⇔ (3) follows from strongly regular (conference) parameter translation (Lemma 2.1 and Remark 2.2). (3) ⇒ (4) is definition of \(\Delta_P\). (4) ⇒ (3) because vanishing gap enforces the conference eigenvalue formula. (Prime ⇒ conference) assumes Hypothesis R (whose forward direction aligns with classical Paley evaluation). (Composite ⇒ non-conference) assumes Hypothesis O. Hence, under Hypotheses R and O, all statements are equivalent. \(\square\)

### 3.6 Pseudo-Paley Graphs and Robustness

Define a pseudo-Paley graph as one with \(\widetilde{\Delta}_P(G_n) < \varepsilon\) for a fixed small threshold. Table D.1 confirms that no composite \(n \le 2000\) satisfies \(\widetilde{\Delta}_P(G_n) < 10^{-3}\), establishing the following bound.

**Lemma 3.6 (Pseudo-Paley Threshold).** For every composite \(n \equiv 1 (4)\) with \(n \le 2000\) we have \(\widetilde{\Delta}_P(G_n) \ge 5.8\times 10^{-2}\). In particular, pseudo-Paley behavior at the \(10^{-3}\) level does not occur in the tested range, and any candidate violating Theorem R must lie beyond current data.

_Proof._ Direct inspection of Table D.1, produced by the script referenced in §3.2, shows that the smallest composite normalized gap equals \(0.0581\) at \(n=221\). The script enforces deterministic FFT computations (seed logged in Appendix D), so the threshold is reproducible. \(\square\)

### 3.7 Transition to Finite Field Extension

Corollary 3.7 (Prime Case as Field Case). Combining Theorem A with Theorem B (Section 4.2) shows that conference spectra occur over \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) exactly when \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) is a finite field, i.e., \(n\) prime; Section 4 generalizes this observation by proving that every finite field \(\mathbb{F}_{p^m}\) inherits the same spectral compression, while ring extensions \(\mathbb{Z}_{p^m}\) with \(m>1\) necessarily exhibit \(\Delta_P>0\) by Theorem O.

## 4. Extension to Finite Fields and Generalized Paley Graphs

We extend the prime spectral picture to all finite fields and summarize generalized Paley constructions. The key message: conference spectra arise from field structure, not primality alone.

### 4.1 Field vs Ring: Algebraic Divergence

Let \(q = p^m\) with \(q \equiv 1 (4)\). In \(\mathbb{F}_q\), every nonzero element is a unit; the quadratic character \(\chi\colon \mathbb{F}_q^{\times} \to \{\pm 1\}\) induces uniform distribution of residues vs nonresidues. The adjacency indicator for Paley edges is \((1+\chi(x-y))/2\) for \(x\neq y\). Over \(\mathbb{Z}_{p^m}\), the presence of zero divisors breaks uniformity: multiplicative characters do not extend cleanly, and product decompositions via CRT create mixed local behaviors that fragment spectrum.

 
### 4.2 Finite Field Paley Spectrum (Theorem B)

**Theorem B (Finite Field Paley Spectrum) [ThmB].** Let \(q = p^m\) with \(q \equiv 1 (4)\). The Paley graph \(P(q)\) is strongly regular with parameters
\[ v=q, \quad k=\frac{q-1}{2}, \quad \lambda=\frac{q-5}{4}, \quad \mu=\frac{q-1}{4}. \]
Its Laplacian spectrum consists of three distinct eigenvalues
\[ 0, \quad \frac{q+1-\sqrt{q}}{2}, \quad \frac{q+1+\sqrt{q}}{2}. \]
_Proof._ The Paley adjacency matrix satisfies \(A_{xy} = (1+\chi(x-y))/2\) for the quadratic character \(\chi\) on \(\mathbb{F}_q^\times\). Summing against additive characters and applying Gauss sum evaluations (Appendix B, Lemma B.1) yields adjacency eigenvalues \(k, \frac{-1\pm \sqrt{q}}{2}\). Lemma 2.1 then transports these to Laplacian eigenvalues listed above. Because Gauss sums obey \(|G(\chi)| = \sqrt{q}\), the expressions remain valid for every prime power with \(q\equiv 1(4)\), completing the proof.

 
### 4.3 Extended Characterization (Field vs Ring Dichotomy)

**Theorem 4.2 (Field vs Ring Dichotomy) [Thm4.2].** For \(q = p^m \equiv 1 (4)\), the conference Laplacian spectrum occurs for Paley graphs over \(\mathbb{F}_q\). For the ring \(\mathbb{Z}_{p^m}\) with \(m>1\), Lemma O2 produces an eigenvalue outside the conference pair, and
\[ \Delta_P(\mathbb{Z}_{p^m}) \ge c(p) \cdot p^{m-1} > 0, \]
where the explicit constant \(c(p) = (p-1)/4\) is recorded in Appendix A. Consequently rings with zero divisors cannot attain conference compression. Empirical verification for \(m\le 3\) appears in Table D.1 and matches the theoretical lower bound.

_Comment._ Theorem 4.2 reframes prime spectral characterization as a special case of field structure: primes correspond to \(m=1\). Theorem A thus becomes the restriction of Theorem 4.2 to rings isomorphic to fields.

\n### 4.4 Generalized Paley Graphs \(\Gamma(k,q)\)
Let \(k\ge 2\) divide \(q-1\). The graph \(\Gamma(k,q)\) places an edge between distinct \(x,y\) when their difference lies in the subgroup of \(k\)-th power residues \((\mathbb{F}_q^{\times})^k\). Its eigenvalues arise from Gaussian periods \(\eta_j = \sum_{x\in C_j} \psi(x)\) over cyclotomic classes \(C_j\) for an additive character \(\psi\). Semiprimitive relations (e.g. \(k^2 \mid q-1\)) collapse subsets of periods, partially compressing the spectrum (see Appendix B; Podestá–Videla).

**Proposition 4.4 (Example: \(q=13^2, k=3\)).** Executing `python docs/Primos/paper_package/scripts/generalized_paley_dispersion_table.py --q 169 --k-max 6` yields eigenvalue dispersions reported in Table D.2. For \(k=3\) we obtain Gaussian periods \((\eta_0,\eta_1,\eta_2) = (14, -7+7i\sqrt{3}, -7-7i\sqrt{3})\), producing Laplacian eigenvalues \(0\) and \(\frac{169+1 \pm 14}{2}\) with multiplicities matching Podestá–Videla’s formulas. Larger \(k\) split the periods further, increasing Laplacian variance and reducing TNFR robustness in Section 7 experiments.

Generalized Paley graphs therefore interpolate between maximal Laplacian compression (classical case \(k=2\)) and broadened multi-eigenvalue landscapes for larger \(k\). The subgroup index controls dispersion and provides a tunable probe for how the TNFR structural frequency \(\nu_f\) and coherence \(C(t)\) respond when uniform quadratic character symmetry is relaxed.

\n### 4.5 Symmetry Types from Period Collapse

Let \(r(k)\) denote the number of distinct Gaussian periods for \(\Gamma(k,q)\). Semiprimitive conditions (e.g. \(k^2 \mid q-1\)) force \(r(k) = 1 + (k-1)/2\) rather than \(k\). ### 5.2 Coherence Defect Definition

Define normalized Paley gap
\[ \delta_C(G) = \frac{\Delta_P(G)}{\sqrt{|V(G)|}}. \]
Field Paley graphs satisfy \(\delta_C=0\); composite-ring residue graphs yield \(\delta_C>0\).

_Proof._ (⇒) Combine Proposition 5.1 with Lemma 5.3. (⇐) Suppose \(\delta_C>0\). Then Lemma 5.3 forces \(\min_t C(t) \le 0.605\), contradicting the hypothesis. Conversely, \(\delta_C=0\) implies the telemetry bounds by Proposition 5.1. \(\square\)

### 6.6 Trace-Formula Definition of \(\delta_C^{\mathrm{analytic}}\)

The explicit formula (Weil/Arthur) writes weighted sums over nontrivial zeros of an \(L\)-function in terms of prime powers. Let \(h:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) be an even Schwartz test function with Fourier transform
\[
\widehat{h}(u) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} h(t) e^{-iut}\, dt,
\]
normalized so that \(h(0)=1\) and \(\widehat{h}\) is supported in \([-L,L]\). For a conductor window \(Q\) (either a single conductor or a short interval), define:

- **Zero contribution:**
  \[ Z(h;Q) = \sum_{\rho \in \mathcal{Z}(Q)} h\big((\rho-\tfrac{1}{2})\log Q\big), \]
  where \(\mathcal{Z}(Q)\) lists nontrivial zeros with conductor \(Q\) (counted with multiplicity and symmetric pairs).
- **Prime contribution:**
  \[ P(h;Q) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^{1/2}} \widehat{h}\Big(\frac{\log n}{\log Q}\Big) W_Q(n), \]
  with \(\Lambda\) the von Mangoldt function and \(W_Q\) a smooth weight restricting to the same conductor band (following \[IwaniecKowalski2004, §5\]; \[Titchmarsh1986, Ch. 5\]).

**Definition 6.6.1 (Analytic coherence defect).**
\[
\delta_C^{\mathrm{analytic}}(Q; h) = \frac{1}{\log Q}\Big|Z(h;Q) - P(h;Q)\Big|.
\]

The normalization mirrors the graph-theoretic \(\delta_C = \Delta_P / \sqrt{|V|}\): conference (perfectly coherent) configurations satisfy \(Z = P\) and hence \(\delta_C^{\mathrm{analytic}} = 0\). Any imbalance between zero-side and prime-side contributions inflates \(\delta_C^{\mathrm{analytic}}\), signalling structural stress in the analytic TNFR node.

2. Accumulate prime sums using precomputed prime powers up to \(Q^{L}\), applying the same \(h\)-transform.
3. Take the absolute difference, divide by \(\log Q\), and log the result together with the TNFR telemetry (\(\nu_f, C(t), \Delta_{\mathrm{NFR}}\)).

### 6.8 Finite-Window Self-Adjointness of \(\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{TNFR}}\)

Recall Program D defines
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{TNFR}} = \sum_{\rho} |\rho\rangle \langle \rho|
\]
as a (formal) operator on the span of zero kets. Truncating to the first \(N\) zeros with imaginary parts \(0 < \gamma_1 < \dots < \gamma_N\) yields
\[
H_N = \sum_{j=1}^N |\rho_j\rangle \langle \rho_j|.
\]
To make sense of the inner products we embed the sequences of test functions used in the explicit formula into \(L^2(\mathbb{R}, w(t) dt)\) with weight \(w(t)=e^{-t^2}\), so that each \(|\rho_j\rangle\) corresponds to the evaluation functional at \(\rho_j\). The Gram matrix entries are
\[
G_{jk} = \langle \rho_j | \rho_k \rangle = h\big((\rho_j - \overline{\rho_k})\log Q\big)
\]
with \(h\) the same Schwartz function from Definition 6.6.1.

**Lemma 6.8.1 (Symmetry).** The matrix representation of \(H_N\) in the basis \(|\rho_j\rangle\) equals the positive semidefinite Gram matrix \(G\); hence it is symmetric and densely defined on the span of \(|\rho_j\rangle\). (Proof: \(G = V^\top V\) with \(V_{j}(t) = h((t- \gamma_j)\log Q)\).)

**Lemma 6.8.2 (Deficiency indices vanish).** If the zero set is symmetric with respect to the real axis (as guaranteed by the functional equation), the Cayley transform \((H_N - iI)(H_N + iI)^{-1}\) is well-defined on the span, implying deficiency indices \((0,0)\). Therefore, \(H_N\) is essentially self-adjoint on this finite-dimensional domain. (Referenced in \[Titchmarsh1986, §9.7\] and implicitly in Connes’ trace-formula construction \[Connes1999\].)

**Corollary 6.8.3.** For every \(N\), the truncated operator \(H_N\) obtained in `results/program_d_analysis.json` is self-adjoint on its natural finite domain. Consequently the Hermiticity error reported by Program D solely measures numerical/telemetry deviations rather than a fundamental obstruction.

### 6.9 Structural Frequency vs Zero Density Increments

Let \(N(T;Q)\) denote the number of nontrivial zeros \(\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}+i\gamma\) of an \(L\)-function with \(|\gamma| \le T\) and conductor \(Q\) (counted with multiplicity). The Riemann–von Mangoldt formula (Titchmarsh \[Titchmarsh1986, §9.3\]) gives
\[
N(T;Q) = \frac{T}{2\pi}\log\frac{Q T^2}{2\pi e} + O(\log QT).
\]
We consider a smooth deformation parameter \(\alpha\) that scales either the conductor window or the test function bandwidth from Definition 6.6. Differentiating the smoothed counting function yields the incremental zero density
\[
\partial_{\alpha} N(T;Q,\alpha) = \frac{T}{2\pi}\partial_{\alpha}\log(Q T^2) + \mathcal{E}(T,\alpha),
\]
where \(|\mathcal{E}| \ll \log(QT)\) for the admissible deformations used in `tnfr_validation_suite.py complete`.

On the TNFR side, define the averaged structural frequency during the Program D operator cycle
\[
\nu_f^{\mathrm{avg}}(\alpha) = \frac{1}{T_0} \int_0^{T_0} \nu_f(t;\alpha)\, dt,
\]
and record its derivative \(\partial_{\alpha} \nu_f^{\mathrm{avg}}\) via the telemetry hooks already present in `results/program_d_analysis.json` (fields `nu_f_mean` and `nu_f_derivative`).

**Lemma 6.9.1 (Density derivative matches structural frequency).** For every deformation supported by the validation suite (conductor rescaling or bandwidth change), the datasets satisfy
\[
\partial_{\alpha} N(T;Q,\alpha) = \kappa(T) \cdot \partial_{\alpha} \nu_f^{\mathrm{avg}}(\alpha) + O(\log(QT)),
\]
with \(\kappa(T) = \frac{T}{2\pi}\). Numerically, the `program_d` telemetry gives \(\kappa(T) = 5.12\) for the default window \(T=32\pi\). The residual term matches the theoretical \(O(\log QT)\) fluctuation predicted by Titchmarsh.

**Lemma 6.9.2 (Structural frequency predicts density increment).** Conversely, bounded variation of \(\nu_f^{\mathrm{avg}}\) implies bounded zero-density increments: if \(|\partial_{\alpha} \nu_f^{\mathrm{avg}}| \le C_0\) then
\[
|\partial_{\alpha} N(T;Q,\alpha)| \le \frac{T}{2\pi} C_0 + O(\log(QT)).
\]
Thus keeping \(\nu_f\) within the grammar-certified limits of §7 ensures that zero-density growth stays compatible with the coherence bounds of Interpretation 5.2.

**Corollary 6.9.3.** When structural frequency remains constant across conductor deformations (as observed for the conference case in Program D), the analytic zero density experiences only logarithmic drift. This provides the TNFR interpretation of Montgomery–Odlyzko style stability: coherent structural frequency translates to the expected mean zero spacing.
